🏗️ Platform
"Our legacy systems are bleeding us dry — but we can't afford to go dark to replace them."
Click to see my approach
1
Diagnose the blast radius first. Map every downstream dependency, failure mode, and integration contract. Categorize systems by risk × replacement complexity — not by age or loudest stakeholder.
2
Strangle, don't rip out. Build a thin abstraction layer that lets new services coexist with legacy. Route traffic incrementally — start with lowest-risk flows, validate, then expand.
3
Make stability a product outcome. Define reliability SLOs, instrument everything with observability, and build executive dashboards that show transformation ROI in real-time — not just delivery velocity.
Outcome: In my past experience, I used this exact approach to consolidate 8 legacy systems with zero service interruption — protecting $5M in annual operational exposure while teams kept shipping.

🤖 AI Strategy
"We want to add AI to our product but don't know where to start without wasting budget."
Click to see my approach
1
Start with the bottleneck, not the technology. Find workflows where humans are doing high-volume, low-judgment work. That's your first AI candidate — not the flashiest use case.
2
Prototype before you architect. Use existing LLM APIs (OpenAI, Claude, Gemini) to validate the use case in days, not months. Measure accuracy and user adoption before buying infrastructure.
3
Define your AI success metrics upfront. Not "we shipped AI" — but task completion rate, time-to-value, error reduction, and cost per interaction. Tie AI to business outcomes from day one.
Outcome: This approach keeps AI investment focused and measurable — avoiding the trap of expensive infrastructure with no adoption or ROI story.
My real example: 73 AI/cloud certifications including Snowflake GenAI (×2), Claude Code (×3), Azure AI, Databricks GenAI, and Agentforce. I've prototyped agent-assisted workflows and know where AI adds signal vs. noise.

🤖 AI Strategy
"Our AI pilots look great in demos but never make it to production."
Click to see my approach
1
Audit the pilot-to-production gap. Usually one of three things: data quality issues hidden in demo environments, no clear owner for productionization, or success criteria that were never defined.
2
Treat every pilot as a product MVP. Set acceptance criteria before building. Define edge cases, failure modes, and monitoring requirements during the pilot phase — not after.
3
Build a clear production readiness checklist. Inference cost, latency benchmarks, fallback behavior, model versioning, and compliance review. If your pilot can't pass it, it wasn't ready to pilot.
Outcome: AI pilots that ship to production, not just PowerPoints. The gap is almost always process and ownership — not technology.
My real example: In my past experience, every automation initiative had defined KPIs, monitoring strategy, and a rollback plan before engineering investment — keeping production incidents minimal.

🤖 AI Strategy
"We're spending heavily on AI tools but seeing no measurable ROI."
Click to see my approach
1
Run an AI spend audit. Map every AI tool to the team using it, the workflow it touches, and the baseline metric before adoption. Most orgs can't answer these — that's the problem.
2
Ruthlessly consolidate. Most AI spend is fragmented across teams buying similar tools independently. Standardize on 2–3 platforms, negotiate enterprise contracts, and build internal enablement so adoption actually sticks.
3
Measure adoption, not licensing. Seats purchased ≠ value delivered. Track weekly active usage, task completion rates, and time-saved per user. ROI comes from adoption — not procurement.
Outcome: Reduced AI tool sprawl, measurable ROI narrative, and a defensible investment roadmap for the next budget cycle.
My real example: In my past experience, I built executive KPI dashboards tracking platform adoption and operational leverage — giving leadership real-time ROI visibility into every major investment.

📈 Scale & Growth
"Onboarding new enterprise customers takes 6+ months — we're losing deals because of it."
Click to see my approach
1
Map the onboarding journey end-to-end. Every handoff, every manual step, every "we email the customer" moment. Time each stage. The longest stages are almost always integration setup and data migration — not configuration.

Productize the top 3 bottlenecks. Build self-service where humans are doing repetitive work. Integration templates, automated data validation, customer-facing status dashboards — these compress months into weeks.
3
Define onboarding SLAs as a product KPI. Track time-to-first-value, time-to-full-activation, and escalation rate by onboarding stage. Make it visible to Sales and CS — not just Implementation.
Outcome: 16+ enterprise implementations per year at Amplity Health — at scale, on time, without growing the implementation headcount proportionally.

"We're losing deals because our product doesn't integrate with what enterprise customers already use."
Click to see my approach
1
Build an integration strategy, not integration projects. Classify your integration landscape: which systems appear in 80% of deals? Those get native connectors. The rest get a documented API contract and partner ecosystem.
2
Invest in API-first architecture. Every core capability should be accessible via API before you build native integrations. This unlocks iPaaS platforms (Workato, Boomi, MuleSoft) as a fast-follow tier for long-tail integrations.
3
Make integrations a sales asset, not an IT afterthought. Publish an integration catalog, track integration adoption by customer, and use it in competitive positioning — "works with your existing stack" closes deals.
Outcome: An integration strategy that scales with your customer base — not one that requires a custom engineering sprint per deal.
My real example: In my past experience, I owned platform integration strategy across Service Cloud, Experience Cloud, and API Gateways — establishing shared integration patterns that eliminated per-team one-off builds.

🚀 Delivery
"We're shipping features constantly but customer adoption is flat. Something's broken."
Click to see my approach
1
Separate output from outcome. Features shipped is a delivery metric, not a product metric. Instrument adoption, engagement depth, and task completion for every shipped feature — within 2 weeks of release.
2
Run an adoption post-mortem on your last 5 releases. For each feature: was the problem validated? Did the solution match how users actually work? Was the release communicated to users? Flat adoption usually traces back to one of these three.
3
Build a closed adoption loop. Discovery → Ship → Measure adoption → Feed findings back into discovery. Not as a process — as a product rhythm. Weekly adoption review, monthly outcome retrospective.
Outcome: Teams that build this loop stop shipping features customers ignore — and start building a backlog of evidence-backed priorities instead of assumptions.
My real example: In my past experience, I used HEART Metrics to track platform adoption, task success, and engagement — feeding telemetry directly into roadmap prioritization and executive investment decisions.

🚀 Delivery
"Our roadmap changes every sprint. No one trusts the plan and teams are burning out."
Click to see my approach
1
Diagnose the source of churn. Is it executives injecting priorities mid-sprint? Is discovery too shallow so requirements change once engineering starts? Is there no buffer for reactive work? Each has a different fix.
2
Establish a capacity allocation model. Explicitly allocate sprint capacity across: strategic initiatives (60%), operational/reactive work (25%), tech debt (15%). When unplanned work comes in, show what it displaces — not just add it to the pile.
3
Create a "roadmap lock" window. The current sprint and next sprint are locked — changes require a formal trade-off decision with business sign-off. Quarter-level priorities are visible to stakeholders 6 weeks ahead. Predictability is built on structure, not heroics.
Outcome: Teams rebuild trust in the plan, reduce context-switching, and delivery predictability improves — usually within 2–3 quarters of consistent application.
My real example: In my past experience, I managed multi-ART delivery across concurrent streams ranging from $200K to $1.3M — with quarterly roadmap commitments visible to senior leadership and protected against mid-sprint scope injection.

🚀 Delivery
"We're always fighting fires and never get to the strategic work that would actually fix things."
Click to see my approach
1
Treat reactive work as a product signal. Every repeated incident, support escalation, and firefight is data about a missing platform capability. Log them, categorize them, and translate the top 3 into strategic investments.
2
Build "reliability headroom" into every planning cycle. If your team spends 30% of time on fires, plan for it — then invest specifically in reducing that number. Make the % visible to leadership as a platform health metric.
3
Shift from reactive to proactive with observability. If you can see problems before they escalate, you can address them during planned work — not emergency response. This is how you escape the fire cycle permanently.
Outcome: In my past experience, shifting from reactive delivery to proactive, observability-driven platform management was the single biggest driver of long-term team throughput.
My real example: Translated operational incidents and support patterns into strategic platform initiatives — reducing P3 escalations by 2% and freeing capacity for the work that actually moved the platform forward.
